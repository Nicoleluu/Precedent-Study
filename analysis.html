Layers of Analysis
Measuring Fairness — Adam Pearce
Ontological Analysis

Measuring Fairness is an interactive web explorable composed of several interconnected layers. At its core are mathematical models that calculate different definitions of fairness, including statistical parity, equal opportunity, and predictive parity. These models are connected to an interactive interface where users manipulate parameters through sliders and controls to observe how fairness metrics change in real time.

The project combines data, algorithms, visualization, explanatory text, and user interaction into a single educational system. Rather than presenting fairness as a fixed answer, it demonstrates that fairness emerges through relationships between datasets, mathematical definitions, and design decisions. The user becomes an active participant by experimenting with these relationships instead of passively reading about them.

Historical & Contextual Analysis

The project was developed by Google PAIR (People + AI Research) during a period when questions surrounding algorithmic bias, AI ethics, and responsible machine learning were becoming increasingly important. Public debates about biased facial recognition systems, hiring algorithms, and criminal risk assessment tools highlighted the need for greater transparency in how machine learning systems make decisions.

Rather than publishing another technical research paper, Google created an interactive educational tool that makes these complex ideas accessible to designers, students, policymakers, journalists, and the general public. The project responds to a growing demand for explainable AI by helping users understand that different mathematical definitions of fairness often conflict with one another, requiring designers to make difficult trade-offs rather than seeking a single correct solution.

Visual & Aesthetic Analysis

The visual language is intentionally minimal and instructional. Clean typography, generous white space, simple color coding, and animated diagrams reduce visual distraction and encourage users to focus on the relationships between variables. Interactive sliders and dynamic charts transform abstract mathematical concepts into visual experiences that can be explored directly.

The project follows the tradition of "explorable explanations," where interaction itself becomes a learning method. Instead of persuading users through static graphics or lengthy text, the interface allows users to test different scenarios and immediately observe how changing one variable affects the overall outcome. This approach situates the project within communities of information visualization, explainable AI, and computational design that use interaction as a primary medium for communicating complex ideas.

01 / Immediate feedback

Every slider movement redistributes errors and recalculates the metrics. The interface makes an abstract statistical relationship feel causal and observable.

02 / Productive failure

Try matching recall, precision, and specificity simultaneously. The difficulty is intentional: the user’s failed attempt becomes evidence for the project’s claim.

03 / Rhetorical framing

The medical scenario gives false positives and false negatives intuitive consequences while presenting ground truth as unusually stable.

04 / Critical limitation

The controlled model explains metric conflict clearly, but it does not represent data collection, institutional power, appeals, or why base rates differ.
