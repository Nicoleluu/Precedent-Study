Research Lineage: Fairness Metrics & Trade-offs

Kleinberg, Jon, Sendhil Mullainathan, and Manish Raghavan. “Inherent Trade-Offs in the Fair Determination of Risk Scores.” 2016.
Formalizes the incompatibility among several reasonable fairness conditions and provides a central theoretical foundation for the explorable.

Chouldechova, Alexandra. “Fair Prediction with Disparate Impact: A Study of Bias in Recidivism Prediction Instruments.” 2016/2017.
Shows why calibration and equal error rates can conflict when groups have different outcome prevalence.

Hardt, Moritz, Eric Price, and Nathan Srebro. “Equality of Opportunity in Supervised Learning.” NeurIPS, 2016.
Introduces equalized odds and equality of opportunity as methods for comparing classification errors across protected groups.

Dwork, Cynthia, Moritz Hardt, Toniann Pitassi, Omer Reingold, and Richard Zemel. “Fairness Through Awareness.” 2012.
Develops an influential individual-fairness principle: similar individuals should receive similar outcomes.

Sociotechnical & Interdisciplinary Critique

Selbst, Andrew D., danah boyd, Sorelle A. Friedler, Suresh Venkatasubramanian, and Janet Vertesi. “Fairness and Abstraction in Sociotechnical Systems.” FAT* ’19, 2019.
Explains how isolating an algorithm from its institutional and social context can undermine fairness analysis. The project links this work in its appendix.

Mulligan, Deirdre K., Joshua A. Kroll, Nitin Kohli, and Richmond Y. Wong. “This Thing Called Fairness: Disciplinary Confusion Realizing a Value in Technology.” 2019.
Compares the different meanings of fairness used across computer science, law, philosophy, and the social sciences.

Historical Context: COMPAS & Public Debate

Angwin, Julia, Jeff Larson, Surya Mattu, and Lauren Kirchner. “Machine Bias.” ProPublica, May 23, 2016.
The investigation that made unequal error rates in criminal-risk prediction a major public controversy.

Larson, Jeff, Surya Mattu, Lauren Kirchner, and Julia Angwin. “How We Analyzed the COMPAS Recidivism Algorithm.” ProPublica, May 23, 2016.
Documents the data and statistical methodology behind ProPublica’s analysis of racial disparities in COMPAS predictions.

Angwin, Julia, and Jeff Larson. “Bias in Criminal Risk Scores Is Mathematically Inevitable, Researchers Say.” ProPublica, December 30, 2016.
Connects the public COMPAS controversy to the emerging mathematical research on incompatible fairness criteria.
